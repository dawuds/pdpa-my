# A1727 in 15 minutes — what actually changed

> **Tier 1 / Lesson 3 — 15 minutes.** A1727 (Personal Data Protection (Amendment) Act 2024) is the biggest PDPA change since the original Act in 2010. Anything written before A1727 is at least partially obsolete.

## Commencement phases

A1727 came into force in three phases. Knowing which phase you are in matters because some obligations are live and some are not yet:

| Phase | Effective | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | 1 February 2025 | Administrative and institutional provisions; Data User → Data Controller terminology relabel |
| Phase 2 | 1 April 2025 | Enhanced penalties; enforcement notice powers; data subject rights amendments (the new rights below) |
| Phase 3 | 1 June 2025 | DPO (s12A), breach notification (s12B), DPIA (s137), RoPA (s138), cross-border transfer reforms (s129A), turnover-based penalties (s130) |

By the time you are reading this in mid-2026, all three phases are in force. A pre-2025 control library that has not been updated for any of the above is broken.

## DPO mandatory under s12A

Pre-A1727, DPO appointment was good practice but not required. A1727 makes it **mandatory for both data controllers and data processors** (s12A). The DPO must be notified to the Commissioner; the notification is via JPDP's SPDP online portal.

The August 2025 DPO Guideline is the operational manual — covers qualifications, independence, reporting line, resources, and the DPO Training Service Providers regime. See [pdpa-my/principles/](../../../principles/) and [Tech-Audit/Regulatory/PDPA/References/Guidelines/JPDP-Guideline-DPO-Appointment-BM-2025.pdf](../../../../Tech-Audit/Regulatory/PDPA/References/Guidelines/JPDP-Guideline-DPO-Appointment-BM-2025.pdf).

Failure to appoint a DPO is a s12A(5) offence: **RM250,000 / 2 years for an individual; RM3M or 10% turnover for the corporate; same exposure for officers personally under s133A**.

## Breach notification under s12B

The single most operationally significant new obligation. **Within 72 hours of becoming aware of a notifiable personal data breach**, the controller must notify the Commissioner. **Within 7 days** the controller must notify affected data subjects where the breach is likely to cause significant harm.

Source for the timings: JPDP DBN Guideline 2025 (s12B itself says "as soon as practicable"; the guideline defines the 72h / 7d clocks).

A notifiable breach is one likely to cause significant harm — financial loss, identity theft, physical harm, reputational damage, loss of confidentiality. Not every minor incident is notifiable; the harm-likelihood assessment is the gating step.

The clash to design around: **NCII clients have a parallel NACSA 6-hour notification under Act 854 s26**. Same incident, two clocks, two regulators. Programme design must reconcile this in advance — see T3 Lesson 5 on the tabletop exercise.

## Data subject rights post-A1727 — three new rights plus two refined

A1727 expanded data subject rights from access + correction (the original s12) to a broader set. Counted strictly: **three new rights** (Erasure s43, Data Portability s43A, Restriction s44) plus **two refined** (Access s30, Correction s34). The corpus uses "three new + two refined" everywhere; older drafts that said "four new rights" were imprecise.

| Right | Section | Practical scope |
|---|---|---|
| Access | s30 (existing, refined) | Receive personal data held about you within 21 days (per Personal Data Protection Regulations 2013) |
| Correction | s34 (existing, refined) | Correct inaccurate data; propagation to third parties to whom previously disclosed |
| **Erasure** | **s43 (NEW)** | "Right to be forgotten" — request deletion where purpose no longer applies, consent withdrawn, processing unlawful |
| **Data Portability** | **s43A (NEW)** | Receive your data in a structured, commonly-used, machine-readable format; controller-to-controller transfer where technically feasible |
| **Restriction of Processing** | **s44 (NEW)** | Restrict (not delete) processing pending dispute resolution — accuracy challenged, processing unlawful but data subject opposes deletion, data needed for legal claims |

Each new right has technical implementation challenges. Portability requires data egress in a standardised format. Erasure requires propagation to backups and third parties. Restriction requires a system flag that prevents processing without deleting the underlying record.

## Sensitive personal data — biometric included (s4)

A1727 amended s4 to include **biometric data** in the definition of sensitive personal data. This means biometric processing (facial recognition, fingerprint authentication, voice biometrics, gait analysis) is now subject to the heightened s40 conditions:

- Explicit consent (`persetujuan secara nyata` — affirmative, specific, informed); OR
- One of the s40(1)(b) grounds: necessary for employment law obligation / vital interests / medical purposes / legal proceedings

Implementation bite: many controllers that deploy biometric authentication did so under "ordinary" consent. Post-A1727 that is insufficient — explicit consent is required, and the consent flow must be redesigned.

## Cross-border transfer reforms (s129A)

Pre-A1727, cross-border transfer required Ministerial whitelist approval or one of the s129(3) limited exceptions (consent, contract necessity, public interest, etc.). The whitelist mechanism was slow and rigid.

A1727 introduced s129A — **new transfer mechanisms**:

- Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)
- Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)
- Certification under a recognised certification scheme
- Specific authorisation by the Commissioner

The JPDP CBPDT Guideline (2025) operationalises this. See T3 Lesson 4.

## Penalty escalation

A1727 substantially raised penalty ceilings. The big-ticket changes:

| Offence | Pre-A1727 | Post-A1727 (individual) | Corporate (s130 NEW) | Officer (s133A NEW) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Principle violation (s5(3)) | RM300K / 2y | RM1M / 3y | RM3M or 10% turnover | Same as corporate |
| Unauthorised data sale (s135A) | — | RM1M / 3y | RM3M or 10% turnover | Same |
| Failure to notify breach (s12B(5)) | n/a | RM500K / 3y | RM3M or 10% turnover | Same |
| Failure to appoint DPO (s12A(5)) | n/a | RM250K / 2y | RM3M or 10% turnover | Same |

The new corporate ceiling (10% Malaysian turnover) and officer personal liability are the figures that escalate boardroom attention. Pre-A1727 a fine was a line-item expense; post-A1727 it is a material risk.

## Terminology: "Data User" → "Data Controller"

Throughout Act 709, "data user" was substituted with "data controller". The post-A1727 reprint of Act 709 (V2022 + amendments) reflects this. Old documents using "data user" are not wrong — they are pre-A1727 vocabulary — but client-facing material drafted post-A1727 should use "data controller".

The processor concept is unchanged in scope but now has its own statutory obligations (s9, s12A).

## GDPR parallel

The major A1727 changes broadly align PDPA with GDPR's existing structure:

| A1727 introduction | GDPR equivalent | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| s12A DPO mandatory | Articles 37-39 DPO | GDPR triggers DPO obligation by activity (Article 37(1)(b)(c)); A1727 makes it broader-scoped — mandatory for all controllers and processors |
| s12B breach notification 72h | Article 33 breach notification 72h | Same clock, different forum (JPDP vs supervisory authority) |
| s43 erasure | Article 17 right to erasure | Direct equivalent |
| s43A data portability | Article 20 right to portability | Direct equivalent |
| s44 restriction | Article 18 restriction | Direct equivalent |
| s129A cross-border mechanisms | Chapter V (SCCs / BCRs / certification) | A1727 imports the GDPR cross-border architecture |

PDPA still lacks GDPR's accountability over-principle (Article 5(2)), but the cumulative effect of A1727 — DPO + RoPA + DPIA + breach notification — creates de facto accountability infrastructure.
