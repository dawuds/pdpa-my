## 1. Purpose

*Describe the overarching purpose of this policy, referencing the applicable legislation and the organization's commitment to lawful cross-border data transfers.*

This policy establishes [Organization Name]'s framework for the lawful, controlled, and accountable transfer of personal data to recipients located outside Malaysia. It gives effect to the obligations imposed by the Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA), as amended by Act A1727, with specific reference to section 129A governing restrictions on the transfer of personal data to places outside Malaysia.

[Organization Name] recognizes that cross-border data flows are integral to modern financial services operations, cloud-based technology adoption, and international business relationships. This policy ensures that all such transfers are conducted with appropriate safeguards, proportionate risk controls, and full compliance with applicable Malaysian law and Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) regulatory expectations.

The objectives of this policy are to:

- Prohibit the unauthorized transfer of personal data outside Malaysia;
- Establish clear criteria for lawful cross-border transfers;
- Define the mechanisms, assessments, and contractual protections required before any transfer may proceed;
- Assign accountability for compliance across all organizational units;
- Ensure ongoing monitoring, documentation, and governance of transfer arrangements; and
- Protect the rights and interests of data subjects whose personal data is transferred internationally.

## 5. Prohibition on Unauthorized Cross-Border Transfers

*This section establishes the default prohibition on cross-border transfers and articulates the conditions under which exceptions are permissible. Reference s129A of the PDPA directly.*

### 5.1 General Prohibition

Pursuant to section 129A of the Personal Data Protection Act 2010, as amended by Act A1727, **[Organization Name] is prohibited from transferring personal data to any place outside Malaysia** unless such transfer satisfies one or more of the conditions specified in this policy and has received prior written approval from the Data Protection Officer (DPO).

No employee, contractor, vendor, or agent of [Organization Name] may initiate, authorize, or facilitate a cross-border transfer of personal data without:

1. Confirming that the destination country or territory is an approved destination (Section 6) or that an approved transfer mechanism is in place (Section 7);
2. Completing a Transfer Impact Assessment where required (Section 8);
3. Ensuring appropriate contractual safeguards are in place (Section 9);
4. Obtaining written authorization from the DPO or their designated delegate; and
5. Recording the transfer in the Register of Cross-Border Transfers (Section 12).

### 5.2 Absolute Prohibitions

Notwithstanding any other provision of this policy, the following transfers are **absolutely prohibited** and may not be authorized under any circumstances:

- Transfer of personal data to any country, territory, or jurisdiction that is subject to international sanctions or that the DPO has determined presents an unacceptable risk to data subjects;
- Transfer of personal data for purposes that are unlawful under Malaysian law or that would facilitate illegal activity;
- Transfer of sensitive personal data without explicit, documented, and informed consent of the data subject, unless an alternative legal basis under the PDPA expressly applies;
- Transfer of personal data to entities that have been blacklisted or debarred by [Organization Name] or any regulatory authority; and
- [Organization Name] to specify any additional absolute prohibitions based on its risk appetite and regulatory environment.]

### 5.3 Consequences of Unauthorized Transfer

Unauthorized cross-border transfers constitute a breach of this policy and may constitute a criminal offence under s136 of the PDPA, exposing [Organization Name] and responsible individuals to:

- Financial penalties as prescribed under the PDPA;
- Regulatory enforcement action by the Personal Data Protection Commissioner;
- Reputational damage and loss of customer trust;
- Civil liability to affected data subjects; and
- Internal disciplinary action up to and including termination of employment.

All suspected or confirmed unauthorized transfers must be reported to the DPO immediately and handled in accordance with [Organization Name]'s Personal Data Breach Management Policy.

## 6. Approved Destination Countries and Adequacy Determinations

*This section lists countries or territories to which personal data may be transferred based on adequacy, and the process for making and reviewing adequacy determinations.*

### 6.1 Adequacy Framework

*Describe the basis on which [Organization Name] determines that a destination country provides adequate protection for personal data.*

A destination country or territory is considered to provide adequate protection where:

- The Minister responsible for the PDPA has issued a determination or order to that effect; or
- [Organization Name]'s DPO, following a documented assessment, has determined that the jurisdiction's data protection laws, regulatory enforcement, and practical data protection environment provide a level of protection substantially equivalent to the PDPA.

Adequacy determinations consider, at minimum:

- The existence and scope of data protection legislation in the destination country;
- The independence and effectiveness of the destination country's data protection authority;
- The accessibility of enforceable data subject rights in the destination country;
- The destination country's international commitments on data protection;
- The track record of data protection compliance in the destination country; and
- Any sector-specific regulations applicable to financial services data in the destination country.

### 6.3 Adequacy Assessment Process

Where a business unit wishes to transfer personal data to a country not listed in Section 6.2, the following process applies:

1. **Request Submission:** The relevant business unit submits a Cross-Border Transfer Request Form (Appendix A) to the DPO, identifying the proposed destination country and the nature of the personal data to be transferred.
2. **Preliminary Assessment:** The DPO conducts a preliminary review of the destination country's legal and regulatory environment within [10] business days.
3. **Full Adequacy Assessment:** Where the preliminary review indicates potential adequacy, the DPO completes a full adequacy assessment using the Adequacy Assessment Template (Appendix B).
4. **DPO Determination:** The DPO issues a written determination within [30] business days of the initial request.
5. **Conditional Approval:** Where adequacy cannot be established, the DPO will advise on alternative transfer mechanisms under Section 7.
6. **Register Update:** Approved adequacy determinations are recorded in the Register of Adequacy Determinations (Appendix C) and the Register of Cross-Border Transfers.

### 6.4 Suspension and Withdrawal of Adequacy

Adequacy determinations may be suspended or withdrawn by the DPO where:

- There is a material change in the destination country's data protection laws or enforcement environment;
- A data breach or incident involving the destination country or a recipient in that country raises concerns about the adequacy of protection;
- The Minister responsible for the PDPA revokes or modifies a relevant determination; or
- Any other circumstance renders the adequacy determination unreliable.

On suspension or withdrawal, all ongoing transfers to the affected country must immediately cease unless an alternative approved transfer mechanism is in place.

**7.2.3 Minimum SCC Requirements**

- Be legally binding on the overseas recipient and enforceable by [Organization Name] and by affected data subjects;
- Require the overseas recipient to process personal data only for the purposes specified;

### 7.4 Data Subject Consent

*Describe when consent may be relied upon as a transfer mechanism and the specific requirements for valid consent in this context.*

**7.4.1 Application**

Consent may be used as the basis for a cross-border transfer only where:

- The data subject has been clearly informed that their personal data will be transferred to a country that may not provide the same level of protection as Malaysia;
- The consent is freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous;
- The data subject has not been subjected to any coercion or negative consequence for refusing consent; and
- The transfer is not so frequent, large-scale, or systematic that it would be impractical or misleading to rely on individual consent.

**7.4.2 Consent Record Requirements**

Where consent is relied upon, [Organization Name] must maintain records demonstrating:

- The identity of the data subject;
- The date and method by which consent was obtained;
- The information provided to the data subject at the time of obtaining consent;
- The specific transfer(s) to which consent applies; and
- Any withdrawal of consent.

**7.4.3 Withdrawal of Consent**

Data subjects have the right to withdraw consent at any time. On withdrawal, [Organization Name] must cease the relevant transfer and, where feasible, arrange for the recall or deletion of the transferred data. Withdrawal does not affect the lawfulness of transfers made before withdrawal.

### 7.5 Contractual Necessity

*Describe when transfers may be made on the basis of contractual necessity.*

A cross-border transfer may be made on the basis of contractual necessity where:

- The transfer is necessary for the performance of a contract between [Organization Name] and the data subject (e.g., facilitating an international payment instruction); or
- The transfer is necessary for the conclusion or performance of a contract made in the interest of the data subject.

This mechanism may not be used where the contractual relationship could feasibly be performed without the cross-border transfer, or where it is used as a pretext to circumvent the general prohibition.

### 7.6 Certification and Approved Codes of Conduct

*Describe the use of certifications or approved codes of conduct as transfer mechanisms.*

Where an overseas recipient holds a certification issued under an internationally recognized data protection certification scheme or adheres to an approved code of conduct that has been assessed by the DPO as providing adequate protection, this may serve as a basis for the transfer subject to:

- The DPO's written confirmation that the certification or code provides equivalent protection to the PDPA;

- Notification to [Organization Name] of any lapse, suspension, or withdrawal of the certification.

### 7.7 Other Permitted Transfer Mechanisms

Transfers may also be permitted in limited circumstances on the basis of:

- **Vital Interests:** Where the transfer is necessary to protect the vital interests of the data subject or another person and the data subject is physically or legally incapable of giving consent.

## 8. Transfer Impact Assessment Requirements

*This section establishes when a TIA is required, what it must cover, and the process for completing and documenting a TIA.*

### 8.1 TIA Obligation

A Transfer Impact Assessment is required before any cross-border transfer is authorized where:

- The destination country has not been assessed as adequate under Section 6;
- The transfer involves sensitive personal data (as defined under s40 of the PDPA) regardless of destination;
- The transfer involves a large volume of personal data (exceeding [threshold, e.g., 10,000 data subjects]);
- The transfer involves personal data of a category presenting elevated risk (e.g., financial data, health data, minor data);
- The transfer involves a new overseas vendor, cloud service provider, or processing arrangement not previously assessed; or
- There has been a material change in circumstances affecting an existing transfer arrangement.

### 8.2 TIA Scope and Content

*Describe the minimum content requirements for a TIA. The TIA is a key accountability document and must be sufficiently detailed to demonstrate that risks have been properly assessed.*

Each TIA must address the following elements as a minimum:

| TIA Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Transfer Description** | Nature, volume, frequency, and categories of personal data to be transferred |
| **Purpose of Transfer** | Legitimate business purpose and legal basis for transfer |
| **Recipient Information** | Identity, location, role, and processing activities of the overseas recipient |
| **Transfer Mechanism** | Applicable mechanism under Section 7 and basis for reliance |
| **Destination Country Assessment** | Assessment of the legal and regulatory environment in the destination country |
| **Legal Access Risks** | Assessment of whether public authorities in the destination country can access the transferred data, and on what legal basis |
| **Data Subject Rights** | Assessment of the practical enforceability of data subject rights in the destination country |
| **Supplementary Measures** | Any technical, contractual, or organizational measures implemented to address identified risks |
| **Residual Risk Assessment** | Assessment of residual risk to data subjects after supplementary measures are applied |
| **DPO Conclusion** | DPO's conclusion on whether the transfer may proceed and any conditions attached |

### 8.3 Supplementary Measures

Where a TIA identifies risks that cannot be adequately addressed by the chosen transfer mechanism alone, [Organization Name] must implement supplementary measures proportionate to the risk identified. Supplementary measures may include:

**Technical Measures:**
- End-to-end encryption using strong encryption standards (minimum AES-256 for data at rest, TLS 1.2 or higher for data in transit);
- Pseudonymization or anonymization of personal data prior to transfer where operationally feasible;
- Data minimization — transferring only the minimum personal data necessary for the purpose;
- Access controls and privileged access management on the recipient's systems; and
- [Other technical measures as determined by TIA findings].

**Contractual Measures:**
- Enhanced contractual obligations on the overseas recipient beyond standard SCCs;
- Audit rights and inspection mechanisms;
- Obligations to notify [Organization Name] of any governmental access requests; and
- Obligations to challenge unlawful governmental access demands.

**Organizational Measures:**
- Enhanced due diligence on the overseas recipient's data protection practices;
- Staff training for recipient personnel handling [Organization Name]'s data;
- Appointment of a dedicated data protection contact at the overseas recipient; and
- Regular compliance reviews and certifications by the recipient.

## 9. Contractual Safeguards for Third-Country Transfers

*This section details the minimum contractual requirements that must be in place before personal data is transferred to third-country recipients.*

### 9.1 Mandatory Contractual Requirements

No cross-border transfer of personal data to an overseas data processor or controller may proceed without a written contract containing, at minimum, the provisions set out in this section. The DPO must review and approve all data processing agreements and cross-border transfer contracts before execution.

### 9.2 Minimum Contract Provisions

All contracts governing cross-border transfers must include the following provisions:

**9.2.1 Data Processing Obligations**

| Clause | Minimum Content |
|---|---|
| Purpose Limitation | Recipient to process personal data only for purposes specified by [Organization Name] |
| Confidentiality | Obligation on recipient's personnel to maintain confidentiality of personal data |
| Security | Recipient to implement technical and organizational security measures at least equivalent to [Organization Name]'s requirements |
| Sub-processing | Prohibition on sub-processing without prior written consent; equivalent obligations on sub-processors |
| Data Subject Rights | Recipient to assist [Organization Name] in responding to data subject rights requests |
| Breach Notification | Recipient to notify [Organization Name] of security breaches within [timeframe, e.g., 24–72 hours] |
| Return/Destruction | Recipient to return or securely delete personal data upon termination |
| Audit Rights | [Organization Name] to have right to audit recipient's compliance |
| Regulatory Cooperation | Recipient to cooperate with Personal Data Protection Commissioner as required |
| Governing Law | Contract governed by Malaysian law or law providing equivalent protections |

**9.2.2 Cross-Border Specific Provisions**

For transfers to countries not assessed as adequate, contracts must additionally include:

- An obligation on the recipient to promptly notify [Organization Name] of any governmental or law enforcement request to access the transferred personal data, to the extent permitted by law;
- An obligation on the recipient to use all available legal remedies to challenge any governmental access request that appears disproportionate or unlawful;
- An obligation on the recipient to maintain the contractual commitments throughout the engagement, including where sub-processors are involved; and
- An obligation to notify [Organization Name] of any material change in the destination country's legal or regulatory environment that may affect the adequacy of protection.

## 10. Employee Responsibilities When Using Overseas Cloud Services

*This section provides specific guidance for employees who use or procure overseas cloud services, SaaS platforms, or other technology services that may involve cross-border data transfers.*

### 10.1 Scope

This section applies to all employees, contractors, and third parties who use cloud computing services, SaaS applications, remote-access tools, or other technology platforms hosted or operated outside Malaysia in connection with [Organization Name]'s personal data.

### 10.2 Prohibited Conduct

The following actions are prohibited without prior DPO approval:

- Uploading, storing, or processing [Organization Name] customer personal data on any overseas cloud platform not approved in accordance with this policy;
- Using personal or non-corporate cloud accounts (e.g., personal Google Drive, Dropbox, iCloud) for storing personal data;
- Using overseas AI tools, large language models, or generative AI platforms in a manner that involves inputting personal data without DPO approval;
- Granting overseas service providers access to [Organization Name]'s systems or data without DPO authorization;
- Circumventing IT controls designed to prevent unauthorized cross-border data flows; and
- Selecting or procuring cloud or overseas technology services involving personal data without completing the vendor assessment and DPO approval process.

### 10.4 Cloud Service Procurement Process

All procurement of cloud services that may involve personal data must follow the process below:

1. **Business Unit Identification:** Business unit identifies requirement for cloud service and completes a Cloud Service Pre-Assessment (Appendix F).
2. **IT Security Review:** IT Security team assesses proposed service for technical security requirements (encryption, access controls, certifications).
3. **DPO Privacy Review:** DPO assesses whether proposed service involves cross-border data transfers and, if so, initiates TIA and contract review processes under Sections 8 and 9.
4. **Vendor Risk Assessment:** Procurement and Risk teams complete vendor due diligence in accordance with [Organization Name]'s Third-Party Risk Management Policy.
5. **BNM Outsourcing Requirements:** Where applicable, Compliance confirms satisfaction of BNM outsourcing notification/approval requirements.
6. **DPO Approval:** DPO issues written approval (or rejection) of the service for use with personal data.
7. **IT Registration:** Approved service is registered in IT asset register and added to the approved cloud services list.
8. **Employee Notification:** Employees are informed of the approved service and any conditions of use.

### 10.5 Employee Responsibilities

All employees must:

- Complete mandatory training on this policy and overseas cloud service use annually;
- Only use approved cloud services (Section 10.3) for processing personal data;
- Report any suspected unauthorized use of overseas cloud services by colleagues to the DPO or through [Organization Name]'s whistleblowing channel;
- Report any suspected data breach or security incident involving overseas cloud services immediately to the IT Security team and DPO;
- Not attempt to circumvent technical controls designed to prevent unauthorized cross-border data flows; and
- Seek guidance from the DPO if uncertain about whether a particular cloud service or activity is permitted.

### 10.6 Bring Your Own Device (BYOD) and Remote Working

Employees working remotely from overseas locations must not access, download, or process [Organization Name] personal data on devices or networks that do not meet [Organization Name]'s security standards. The IT Security team must be notified of any extended overseas working arrangement involving access to personal data.

## 11. Ongoing Monitoring of Transfer Arrangements

*This section establishes requirements for the continuous oversight of cross-border transfer arrangements to ensure they remain lawful and effective.*

### 11.1 Monitoring Obligations

[Organization Name] is committed to the ongoing oversight of all active cross-border transfer arrangements. Monitoring is necessary to ensure that:

- Transfer arrangements continue to be lawful as circumstances evolve;
- Changes in destination country laws or the recipient's circumstances are identified promptly;
- Overseas recipients remain compliant with their contractual obligations;
- New risks to personal data are identified and addressed; and
- The Register of Cross-Border Transfers remains accurate and current.

### 11.2 Monitoring Activities

The DPO is responsible for coordinating the following monitoring activities:

| Activity | Frequency | Responsible Party |
|---|---|---|
| Review of Register of Cross-Border Transfers for completeness and accuracy | Quarterly | DPO |
| Review of adequacy determinations for destination countries | Annual (minimum) | DPO |
| Review of TIAs for ongoing relevance | Annual or upon material change | DPO |
| Vendor compliance review / audit of key overseas processors | Annual | DPO / IT Security / Procurement |
| Review of approved cloud services list | Annual | DPO / IT |
| Assessment of changes in destination country data protection laws | Ongoing / as events arise | DPO |
| Review of data breach incidents involving overseas recipients | Following any incident | DPO / IT Security |
| Staff training effectiveness review | Annual | DPO / HR |
| Review of contractual safeguards with overseas processors | Annual or upon contract renewal | DPO / Legal |
| Full policy review | Annual | DPO |

### 11.3 Triggering Events for Out-of-Cycle Review

The following events require an immediate out-of-cycle review of affected transfer arrangements:

- Notification of a data breach or security incident at an overseas recipient;
- Discovery of unauthorized processing by an overseas recipient;
- Material change in the data protection laws of a destination country;
- Regulatory action by a destination country's data protection authority affecting an overseas recipient;
- Governmental or law enforcement access to personal data transferred by [Organization Name];
- Change of control, merger, or acquisition affecting an overseas recipient;
- Withdrawal or suspension of a certification or code of conduct relied upon as a transfer mechanism; and
- Any other event that may materially affect the adequacy of protection for transferred personal data.

### 11.4 Vendor Audit Programme

[Organization Name] maintains a risk-based audit programme for overseas data processors. The programme is administered by the DPO in coordination with IT Security and Procurement:

| Vendor Category | Audit Frequency | Audit Method |
|---|---|---|
| Critical / High Risk (large volume, sensitive data) | Annual | On-site audit / third-party audit report / detailed questionnaire |
| Medium Risk | Every [2] years | Questionnaire + document review |
| Low Risk | Every [3] years | Self-assessment questionnaire |

Audit findings are reported to the DPO and senior management. Material findings requiring remediation are tracked through to closure.

### 11.5 Escalation and Suspension

Where monitoring activities reveal that an ongoing transfer arrangement no longer provides adequate protection for personal data, the DPO must:

1. Immediately assess whether the transfer should be suspended;
2. Where the risk is serious and imminent, suspend the transfer without delay and notify affected business units;
3. Notify the Personal Data Protection Commissioner where required;
4. Engage with the overseas recipient to remediate identified issues;
5. Determine whether the transfer arrangement can resume and on what basis; and
6. Document all actions taken and outcomes in the TIA Register and Incident Log.
