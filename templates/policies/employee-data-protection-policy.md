## 1. Purpose

*This section should articulate why this policy exists, the specific harm it mitigates, and the organizational commitment it embodies. Reference the statute and any sector-specific guidance applicable to the organization.*

This policy establishes the requirements, standards, and controls that govern how [Organization Name] collects, uses, stores, discloses, transfers, and disposes of the personal data of individuals throughout the employment lifecycle. It reflects [Organization Name]'s commitment to handling such data responsibly, lawfully, and transparently, in full compliance with the **Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA)** and all subsidiary legislation, guidelines, and codes of practice issued thereunder.

The specific purposes of this policy are to:

- Define the categories of personal data collected and processed in connection with employment;
- Establish the lawful bases under which employee personal data is processed;
- Set out the data protection rights of individuals covered by this policy and the mechanisms through which those rights may be exercised;
- Articulate the obligations and responsibilities of [Organization Name] and its personnel in relation to employee data protection;
- Prescribe minimum standards for the security, retention, and disposal of employee personal data;
- Ensure that monitoring and surveillance activities in the workplace are proportionate, disclosed, and lawfully conducted;
- Support [Organization Name]'s accountability obligations and its readiness for regulatory audit or investigation.

Compliance with this policy is mandatory for all personnel who handle, access, or make decisions about employee personal data.

## 4. Definitions

*Provide clear, PDPA-consistent definitions for all key terms used in this policy. Definitions should mirror statutory language where possible to avoid interpretive inconsistency.*

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Personal Data** | Any information in respect of commercial transactions that relates directly or indirectly to a data subject, who is identified or identifiable from that information or from that and other information in the possession of a data controller, including any sensitive personal data and expression of opinion about the data subject (PDPA, s. 4) |
| **Sensitive Personal Data** | Personal data consisting of information about the physical or mental health or condition of a data subject; his political opinions; his religious beliefs or other beliefs of a similar nature; the commission or alleged commission of any offence; or any other personal data as the Minister may determine (PDPA, s. 4) |
| **Data Controller** | A person who either alone, or jointly, or in common with other persons, processes any personal data or has control over, or authorizes, the processing of any personal data. [Organization Name] is a data controller in respect of employee personal data |
| **Data Processor** | Any person, other than an employee of the data controller, who processes the personal data solely on behalf of the data controller, and does not process the personal data for any of his own purposes |
| **Processing** | Collecting, recording, holding, or storing personal data or carrying out any operation or set of operations on personal data — including organizing, adapting, altering, retrieving, combining, transmitting, erasing, or destroying it |
| **Data Subject** | An individual who is the subject of personal data. In this policy, data subjects are Covered Persons as defined in Section 2.1 |
| **Consent** | Any freely given, specific, and informed indication of a data subject's wishes by which, after being provided a written notice, the data subject signifies agreement to personal data relating to him being processed |
| **DPO** | Data Protection Officer — the individual designated by [Organization Name] to oversee data protection compliance |
| **Employment Lifecycle** | The full continuum from pre-employment (recruitment and selection) through active employment to post-employment (offboarding and alumni management) |
| **HR Systems** | All information systems, databases, and platforms used to manage employee data, including but not limited to HRIS, payroll systems, performance management platforms, and learning management systems |
| **Pseudonymisation** | The processing of personal data in such a manner that the data can no longer be attributed to a specific data subject without the use of additional information kept separately and subject to technical and organisational measures |

## 6. Lawful Bases for Employee Data Processing

*This is a critical section for PDPA compliance. The PDPA's General Principle (s. 6) requires that personal data is not processed without consent, but provides lawful exceptions. Map every material processing activity to its lawful basis. Avoid over-reliance on consent in employment contexts, where consent may not be freely given.*

### 6.1 Overview of Lawful Bases

Under PDPA Section 6, [Organization Name] relies on the following lawful bases for processing employee personal data:

| Lawful Basis | PDPA Reference | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Consent** | s. 6(1) | The data subject has given consent to the processing of their personal data for one or more specific purposes |
| **Contract** | s. 6(1)(b) | Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract to which the data subject is a party, or to take steps at the request of the data subject prior to entering into a contract |
| **Legal obligation** | s. 6(1)(c) | Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation to which the data controller is subject |
| **Vital interests** | s. 6(1)(d) | Processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of the data subject |
| **Legitimate interests** | s. 6(1)(f) | Processing is necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the data controller or by a third party, except where such interests are overridden by the interests or fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subject |

### 6.2 Processing Activity to Lawful Basis Mapping

*Complete this table for all material processing activities. This mapping forms part of the Record of Processing Activities (RoPA) and should be reviewed annually or upon any change to processing activities.*

| Processing Activity | Lawful Basis | Supporting Rationale / Statutory Reference |
|---|---|---|
| Recruitment and selection | Contract (pre-contractual steps) | Processing candidate data is necessary to assess suitability and take steps to enter an employment contract |
| Payroll administration | Contract; Legal obligation | Contract performance; Employment Act 1955, Income Tax Act 1967, EPF Act 1991, SOCSO Act 1969 |
| EPF and SOCSO contributions | Legal obligation | Employees Provident Fund Act 1991 (Act 452); Employees' Social Security Act 1969 (Act 4) |
| Tax deduction at source (MTD/PCB) | Legal obligation | Income Tax Act 1967 |
| Immigration and work pass management | Legal obligation; Contract | Immigration Act 1959/63; contractual necessity |
| Performance management | Contract; Legitimate interests | Contract performance; legitimate organizational interests in workforce development |
| Disciplinary proceedings | Legitimate interests; Legal obligation | Fair employment practices; Employment Act 1955 |
| Health and safety records | Legal obligation | Occupational Safety and Health Act 1994 (Act 514) |
| Employee benefits administration | Contract; Consent (for optional benefits) | Contract terms; voluntary enrolment requires consent |
| Workplace monitoring (IT systems) | Legitimate interests | Protecting organizational assets; disclosed in advance — see Section 9 |
| Background screening | Legitimate interests; Consent | Risk management for regulated roles; applicant consent obtained pre-screening |
| Exit and offboarding | Contract; Legal obligation | Contract wind-down; record-keeping obligations |
| Reference provision post-employment | Legitimate interests | Legitimate interest in verifying employment history; minimal data disclosed |

> **Guidance for Policy Authors:** Where [Organization Name] relies on **legitimate interests**, a Legitimate Interests Assessment (LIA) must be documented and retained. The LIA must demonstrate that the legitimate interest is genuine, that processing is necessary, and that the individual's interests do not override the legitimate interest. Contact the DPO to initiate an LIA.

### 6.3 Special Considerations Regarding Consent in Employment

*This subsection addresses the power imbalance inherent in employment relationships, which may compromise the validity of consent as a lawful basis.*

[Organization Name] acknowledges that, in the employment context, consent may not always constitute a freely given, valid lawful basis under PDPA, as employees may feel compelled to consent due to the imbalance of power in the employment relationship. Accordingly:

- [Organization Name] will **not** rely primarily on consent for processing activities that are necessary for the performance of the employment contract or compliance with legal obligations;
- Where consent is used as the lawful basis, [Organization Name] will ensure that:
  - The consent request is separate from the employment contract;
  - Refusal to consent does not result in any detriment to the employee;
  - Consent can be withdrawn at any time without adverse consequence;
  - A record of consent given is maintained.
- Processing activities for which consent is used as the lawful basis are limited to: [List specific activities, e.g., optional wellness programmes, alumni communications, photograph use in internal directories];
- Where an employee withdraws consent, [Organization Name] will cease the relevant processing activity within [X] business days, unless an alternative lawful basis applies.

## 7. Employee Data Protection Notice Requirements

*PDPA Section 7 (Notice and Choice Principle) requires data controllers to inform data subjects of the matters listed in s. 7(1) before or at the time of collection. This section specifies how [Organization Name] fulfils that obligation across the employment lifecycle.*

### 7.1 Obligation to Issue Written Notice

Prior to or at the point of collecting personal data from any Covered Person, [Organization Name] must provide a written data protection notice ("**Privacy Notice**") that includes, at minimum, the information required under PDPA Section 7(1):

- A description of the personal data being collected;
- The purpose(s) for which the personal data is being, or may be, processed;
- Any information available to [Organization Name] as to the source of the personal data (if not collected directly from the data subject);
- The data subject's right to request access to and correction of their personal data, and how to exercise those rights;
- The data subject's right to limit the processing of their personal data;
- The name and contact details of the Data Protection Officer or designated contact point;
- Whether it is obligatory or voluntary for the data subject to supply the personal data, and if obligatory, the consequences of failing to supply it;
- The class(es) of third parties to whom the personal data may be disclosed.

### 7.2 Privacy Notices by Lifecycle Stage

*A separate, tailored Privacy Notice must be prepared and issued for each lifecycle stage. Generic omnibus notices are discouraged and may not meet the specificity requirement of s. 7.*

| Lifecycle Stage | Privacy Notice | Delivery Mechanism | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| Recruitment | Applicant Privacy Notice | Embedded in job application form / careers portal | At point of application |
| Pre-employment checks | Background Check Consent and Notice | Issued separately during offer stage | Before checks commence |
| Onboarding | Employee Privacy Notice | Included in onboarding pack; acknowledged in HRIS | Day 1, before data collection begins |
| Contractor engagement | Contractor Data Protection Notice | Included in contractor agreement / onboarding | Before engagement commences |
| Workplace monitoring | Monitoring Disclosure Notice | Included in employment contract, Acceptable Use Policy, and posted at monitored locations | Before monitoring commences |
| Post-employment | Former Employee Privacy Notice | Issued at offboarding | Before or at departure |

### 7.3 Content Requirements for Employee Privacy Notice

*The Employee Privacy Notice is the primary disclosure mechanism for active employees. It must be reviewed annually and updated whenever processing activities change materially.*

The Employee Privacy Notice issued at onboarding must address, at minimum, the following:

- [ ] Identity and contact details of [Organization Name] as data controller;
- [ ] Identity and contact details of the DPO;

[Organization Name] must retain evidence that each data subject has received and acknowledged the applicable Privacy Notice. The preferred mechanism for acknowledgement is:

## 8. Employee Rights Under PDPA

*PDPA Section 12 (Access Principle) grants data subjects the right to access and correct their personal data. The PDPA also provides a right to withdraw consent and to limit processing in specified circumstances (s. 7(3) and s. 38). This section operationalises those rights.*

### 8.1 Summary of Employee Rights

| Right | PDPA Reference | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Right of Access** | s. 12(1)(a) | The right to be informed whether [Organization Name] holds personal data about the employee, and if so, to be supplied with a copy of that data |
| **Right of Correction** | s. 12(1)(b) | The right to have inaccurate, incomplete, misleading, or not up-to-date personal data corrected |
| **Right to Limit Processing** | s. 7(3) | The right to withdraw consent or to request that [Organization Name] cease or not begin processing personal data that causes or is likely to cause unwarranted and substantial damage or distress |

| **Right to Withdraw Consent** | s. 6 | Where consent is the lawful basis, the right to withdraw consent at any time |

## 9. Monitoring in the Workplace

*Workplace monitoring is a significant data protection concern. PDPA's Notice and Choice Principle (s. 7), General Principle (s. 6), and Disclosure Principle (s. 8) all apply. This section establishes the conditions under which monitoring is permissible, the disclosure requirements, and the prohibited forms of monitoring.*

### 9.1 Policy Statement on Monitoring

[Organization Name] recognizes employees' reasonable expectations of privacy in the workplace and is committed to ensuring that any monitoring of employees is:

- **Lawful** — conducted on a lawful basis under PDPA and any other applicable law;
- **Proportionate** — limited to what is necessary for the legitimate purpose stated;
- **Transparent** — disclosed to employees in advance through appropriate notices;
- **Purpose-limited** — data gathered through monitoring is used only for the stated purpose.

Monitoring of employees without prior disclosure is strictly prohibited, except where [Organization Name] has reasonable grounds to suspect serious misconduct and has obtained prior authorization from [specify — e.g., CEO and Legal Counsel].

## 10. HR System Access Controls and Data Segregation

*PDPA Section 9 (Security Principle) requires data controllers to take practical steps to protect personal data from loss, misuse, modification, unauthorized access or disclosure, and alteration. This section operationalises that obligation for HR systems.*

### 10.1 HR System Inventory

*The DPO and HR Director are jointly responsible for maintaining an accurate and current inventory of all systems that process employee personal data. This inventory should align with the organization's IT asset register.*

| [Employee Wellness Platform] | Wellness | Health survey data, EAP usage | Third-party SaaS | HR Director |

### 10.2 Access Control Principles

Access to HR systems and employee personal data shall be governed by the following principles:

- **Need-to-know / Least privilege:** Access to employee personal data is granted only to individuals who require it to perform their specific job function. No user shall have access beyond what is necessary for their role;
- **Role-based access control (RBAC):** Access is granted based on job role, not individual, to facilitate consistent provisioning and de-provisioning;
- **Segregation of duties:** No single individual should have unrestricted access to all aspects of the HR data lifecycle (e.g., the person who configures payroll should not also be the person who authorizes payroll runs);
- **Privileged access management:** Super-user and administrator accounts must be subject to additional controls, including multi-factor authentication (MFA) and access logging.

### 10.3 Access Control Matrix

*Segregation prevents unauthorized lateral access and limits the blast radius of any single access control failure.*

[Organization Name] shall maintain logical and, where practicable, physical segregation of the following categories of employee data:

- **Background check results** must be stored by the authorized background check provider and accessed only by designated HR personnel; results must not be retained in the general HR record beyond the period necessary for the employment decision.

## 11. Employee Data Retention Schedule and Disposal

*PDPA Section 10 (Retention Principle) prohibits retaining personal data for longer than necessary. A documented and enforced retention schedule is a primary control for compliance with this principle and demonstrates accountability.*

### 11.1 Retention Principles

[Organization Name] shall retain employee personal data only for as long as is necessary for the purposes for which it was collected, or as required by applicable law, regulation, or legitimate business need. Upon expiry of the applicable retention period, personal data must be securely destroyed or permanently anonymised.

Retention periods are determined by reference to:

- **Statutory requirements** — e.g., retention periods mandated by the Employment Act 1955, Income Tax Act 1967, Limitation Act 1953;
- **Regulatory requirements** — e.g., requirements imposed by BNM or sector regulators;
- **Contractual requirements** — e.g., obligations under collective agreements or audit commitments;
- **Litigation hold** — data subject to a litigation hold must not be destroyed until the hold is lifted by Legal Counsel.

## 14. Breach Notification and Incident Response

*PDPA does not currently mandate breach notification to the PDPC as a general rule, but voluntary notification and internal incident response are best-practice obligations. Monitor for legislative updates.*

### 14.1 Definition of a Personal Data Breach

A personal data breach is any security incident that results in:

- Unauthorised access to, or acquisition of, employee personal data;
- Accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration of employee personal data;
- Unauthorised disclosure of employee personal data;
- Any other processing of employee personal data that violates this policy or applicable law.

### 14.2 Incident Response Procedure

| Phase | Action | Responsible | Timeline |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Detection** | Identify and report suspected or confirmed breach to DPO | Any employee / IT / HR | Immediately upon discovery |
| **Containment** | Implement immediate containment measures to prevent further data exposure | IT / CISO | Within [2] hours of notification |
| **Assessment** | Assess the nature, scope, and potential impact of the breach; identify affected data subjects | DPO / IT / HR / Legal | Within [24] hours |
| **Notification — Internal** | Brief senior management on breach scope and response | DPO | Within [24] hours |
| **Notification — Regulator** | Consider voluntary notification to PDPC; notify if legally required under any applicable sector regulation | DPO / Legal | Within [72] hours (align with best practice) |
| **Notification — Data Subjects** | Notify affected data subjects where breach is likely to result in high risk to their rights and interests | DPO / Legal | As soon as practicable |
| **Remediation** | Implement corrective and preventive actions; update controls as necessary | DPO / IT / HR | Per remediation plan |
| **Post-Incident Review** | Conduct post-incident review; document lessons learned | DPO / IT / HR | Within [30] days of resolution |

### 14.3 Breach Register

The DPO shall maintain a Breach Register documenting all personal data incidents, including those that do not result in regulatory notification. The Breach Register shall record:

- Date and description of the incident;
- Categories and approximate volume of personal data affected;
- Immediate and long-term impact assessment;
- Containment and remediation actions taken;
- Regulatory and data subject notification decisions and outcomes.

## 15. Third-Party and Processor Obligations

*Under PDPA, the data controller remains responsible for personal data processed by third parties on its behalf. Contractual controls over processors are essential.*

### 15.1 Vendor Due Diligence

Before engaging any third party to process employee personal data on behalf of [Organization Name], the DPO and the relevant business owner must:

- Conduct data protection due diligence on the proposed processor, including review of the processor's privacy policy, security certifications, and data processing practices;
- Assess whether the processor provides sufficient guarantees regarding technical and organizational security measures;
- Obtain approval from the DPO and, for sensitive personal data, from Legal Counsel.

### 15.2 Data Processing Agreements

[Organization Name] must enter into a written Data Processing Agreement (DPA) with every third party that processes employee personal data on its behalf. The DPA must require the processor to:

- Process personal data only on [Organization Name]'s documented instructions;
- Ensure personnel authorized to process personal data are subject to confidentiality obligations;
- Implement appropriate technical and organisational security measures;
- Assist [Organization Name] in fulfilling data subject rights requests;
- Not engage sub-processors without prior written consent from [Organization Name];
- Delete or return all personal data upon termination of the engagement;
- Make available to [Organization Name] all information necessary to demonstrate compliance with data protection obligations;
- Allow for and contribute to audits or inspections.

### Appendix F — Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA) Trigger List

*A DPIA should be conducted before commencing any new or changed processing activity that is likely to result in high risk to individuals' rights and interests. The following are indicative triggers.*

A DPIA is required before [Organization Name] commences any processing activity that involves one or more of the following:

- [ ] Systematic monitoring of employees (e.g., introduction of new workplace monitoring technology);
- [ ] Processing of sensitive personal data at scale (e.g., introduction of a new health and wellbeing platform);
- [ ] Automated decision-making that significantly affects employees (e.g., algorithmic performance scoring);
- [ ] Processing involving new technologies or novel uses of existing technology;
- [ ] Processing data of a particularly sensitive nature in combination (e.g., combining health data with location data);
- [ ] Sharing employee data with a new third-party processor or transferring data to a new jurisdiction;
- [ ] Any processing activity identified by the DPO as carrying elevated risk.

DPIAs must be initiated by the relevant business owner, conducted in consultation with the DPO, and approved by senior management before processing commences.
