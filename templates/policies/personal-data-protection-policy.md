## 1. Purpose

*This section states why this policy exists and what problem or obligation it addresses. Authors should describe the organisation's commitment to personal data protection and the regulatory drivers behind this policy.*

This Personal Data Protection Policy ("Policy") establishes the principles, obligations, governance framework, and operational requirements by which [Organization Name] ("the Organisation") collects, processes, holds, transfers, and disposes of personal data. The Policy reflects the Organisation's commitment to protecting the privacy and rights of individuals whose personal data is handled in the course of business operations.

This Policy is established in accordance with the **Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (Act 709)** ("PDPA") and its subsidiary legislation, as amended by the **Personal Data Protection (Amendment) Act 2024 (Act A1727)** ("the 2024 Amendment"). It operationalises the seven data protection principles prescribed under the PDPA and gives effect to the Organisation's legal, ethical, and reputational obligations as a data user.

The Policy serves the following purposes:

- To ensure that personal data processed by the Organisation is handled lawfully, fairly, and transparently.
- To assign clear accountability for personal data protection across all business functions and levels of the Organisation.
- To establish minimum standards for the collection, processing, storage, transfer, and destruction of personal data.
- To protect the rights of data subjects, including customers, employees, and third parties.
- To enable the Organisation to demonstrate compliance with the PDPA to regulators, auditors, and stakeholders.
- To reduce the risk of personal data breaches and to establish clear response procedures when breaches occur.

## 2. Scope

*This section defines who and what is covered by the policy. Authors should specify business units, data categories, and any exclusions.*

### 2.1 Organisational Scope

This Policy applies to:

- **All employees** of [Organization Name], including permanent staff, contract staff, temporary workers, interns, and seconded personnel.
- **The Board of Directors** and senior management in their exercise of oversight responsibilities.
- **All subsidiaries and affiliates** of [Organization Name] operating in Malaysia, unless a separate compliant policy is maintained by that entity.
- **Third-party service providers, vendors, contractors, and agents** who process personal data on behalf of the Organisation, to the extent specified in their contractual agreements.
- **All business units, departments, and functions** that collect, process, store, transfer, or dispose of personal data in any format (electronic, paper-based, or otherwise).

### 2.2 Data Scope

This Policy applies to all **personal data** as defined under section 4 of the PDPA, processed in whole or in part by means within Malaysia. This includes but is not limited to:

- Personal data of customers (retail, corporate, and SME) and prospective customers.
- Personal data of employees and job applicants.
- Personal data of vendors, counterparties, and business contacts.
- Personal data of guarantors, beneficial owners, and authorised representatives.
- **Sensitive personal data** as defined under section 4 and regulated under section 12 of the PDPA, including financial data, health information, and biometric data where applicable.

### 2.3 Exclusions

This Policy does not apply to:

- Personal data processed by an individual for personal, family, or household affairs (domestic purposes).
- Personal data processed for credit reference purposes to the extent governed by separate instruments.
- Data that has been fully and irreversibly anonymised such that no individual can be identified, directly or indirectly.

## 6. Seven Data Protection Principles — Obligations Summary

*This section summarises the Organisation's obligations under each of the seven data protection principles in the PDPA. For each principle, authors should document specific operational requirements. The PDPA prescribes seven data protection principles with which the Organisation, as a data user, must comply. The following subsections summarise the Organisation's interpretation of, and obligations under, each principle.

### 6.1 General Principle (PDPA Section 5)

*Summarise the Organisation's approach to ensuring that personal data is processed only with consent or a lawful basis, and only for the specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes for which it was collected.*

**Statutory Requirement (s5):** A data user shall not process personal data about a data subject unless the data subject has given consent to the processing of the personal data, except in the circumstances permitted by section 5(2).

**Organisational Obligations:**

- The Organisation shall process personal data only for purposes that have been specified, explicit, and legitimate at the time of collection.
- Consent shall be obtained from data subjects prior to, or at the time of, collection of personal data, using a mechanism that provides a genuine choice and is documented and auditable.
- Where consent is relied upon as the legal basis for processing, the Organisation shall maintain records of consent, including what the data subject consented to, when, and through what mechanism.
- The Organisation shall not process personal data for purposes incompatible with those originally specified without obtaining fresh consent or identifying an applicable exception under section 5(2).
- Reliance on lawful processing bases other than consent (e.g., contractual necessity, legal obligation, legitimate interests as applicable) shall be documented and approved by the Data Protection Officer.

**Permitted Processing Without Consent (s5(2)):**

| Circumstance | Conditions |
|---|---|
| Performance of a contract | The processing is necessary for the performance of a contract to which the data subject is a party |
| Legal obligation | The processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation to which the data user is subject |
| Vital interests | The processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of the data subject |
| Administration of justice | The processing is necessary for the administration of justice |
| Exercise of statutory functions | The processing is necessary for the exercise of any functions conferred on any person by law |
| Legitimate interests | [To be updated to reflect the current PDPA text and any amendments under Act A1727] |

### 6.2 Notice and Choice Principle (PDPA Section 6)

*Summarise the Organisation's approach to informing data subjects about the collection and processing of their personal data, and to giving them a meaningful choice.*

**Statutory Requirement (s6):** A data user shall inform the data subject of specified matters before collecting or processing personal data about the data subject.

**Organisational Obligations:**

- The Organisation shall provide clear, accessible, and plain-language privacy notices to data subjects at or before the point of collection of personal data.
- Privacy notices shall contain, at minimum:
  - The description of personal data being processed.
  - The purpose(s) for which the personal data is being processed.
  - The class(es) of third parties to whom personal data may be disclosed.
  - Whether the supply of personal data is obligatory or voluntary, and the consequences of failure to supply.
  - The rights of the data subject under the PDPA to access and correct personal data.
  - Contact details of the Data Protection Officer or designated contact point.
- Where personal data is collected indirectly (from third parties), the Organisation shall take reasonable steps to ensure the data subject has been notified of the processing.
- The Organisation shall maintain a current, publicly accessible **Privacy Notice** on its website and in its branch or service facilities.
- Data subjects shall be offered a genuine and meaningful choice to withdraw consent where consent is the basis for processing, without detriment where lawful to do so.

### 6.3 Disclosure Principle (PDPA Section 7)

*Summarise the Organisation's restrictions on disclosing personal data to third parties, including requirements for consent and contractual controls.*

**Statutory Requirement (s7):** A data user shall not disclose personal data to any person other than the data subject without the consent of the data subject, except in the circumstances permitted by section 7.

**Organisational Obligations:**

- Personal data shall not be disclosed to third parties without the prior consent of the data subject, except where a permitted disclosure ground under section 7 applies.
- All third-party disclosures shall be documented in the Organisation's **Data Sharing Register** (see Appendix C).
- Where personal data is shared with data processors (e.g., cloud service providers, outsourced service providers), the Organisation shall execute a **Data Processing Agreement** containing obligations equivalent to those imposed on the Organisation by the PDPA.
- Cross-border transfers of personal data shall only be made to countries that provide a standard of protection for personal data no less than the standard required under the PDPA, or where an adequate safeguard (e.g., contractual clauses, consent) is in place, in accordance with section 129 of the PDPA.
- The Legal and Compliance function shall review and approve all new categories of third-party personal data disclosure prior to commencement.

### 6.5 Retention Principle (PDPA Section 9)

*Summarise the Organisation's approach to ensuring personal data is not retained longer than is necessary for the purposes for which it was collected.*

**Statutory Requirement (s9):** Personal data processed for any purpose shall not be kept longer than is necessary for the fulfilment of that purpose.

**Organisational Obligations:**

- The Organisation shall maintain a **Data Retention Schedule** (see Appendix D) that specifies, for each category of personal data, the minimum and maximum retention periods, the legal or regulatory basis for the retention period, and the method of disposal at end of retention.
- Personal data shall be deleted, destroyed, or anonymised promptly upon expiry of the applicable retention period, unless a legal hold or regulatory requirement requires extended retention.
- Retention periods shall account for applicable legal obligations (e.g., Anti-Money Laundering Act, Financial Services Act) and regulatory requirements that mandate minimum retention of records.
- The Organisation shall implement automated or procedural controls to identify and action personal data that has reached the end of its retention period.
- Any extension of a retention period beyond the standard retention schedule shall be documented, approved by the Data Protection Officer or Legal function, and recorded in the Data Retention Register.

### 6.6 Data Integrity Principle (PDPA Section 10)

*Summarise the Organisation's approach to maintaining the accuracy, completeness, and currency of personal data.*

**Statutory Requirement (s10):** A data user shall take reasonable steps to ensure that the personal data is accurate, complete, not misleading, and kept up to date by having regard to the purpose, including any directly related purpose, for which the personal data was collected and further processed.

**Organisational Obligations:**

- The Organisation shall implement processes to verify the accuracy and completeness of personal data at the point of collection and throughout the data lifecycle.
- The Organisation shall provide data subjects with accessible mechanisms to update and correct their personal data (see Section 8 — Data Subject Rights).
- Front-line staff shall be trained to record personal data accurately and to flag discrepancies or errors promptly.
- Systems holding personal data shall include data validation controls to reduce the risk of inaccurate data entry.
- The Organisation shall conduct periodic data quality reviews of its key personal data repositories to identify and remediate inaccurate, incomplete, or outdated records.
- Where inaccurate personal data has been disclosed to a third party, the Organisation shall take reasonable steps to inform that third party of the correction, where practicable.

### 6.7 Access Principle (PDPA Section 11)

*Summarise the Organisation's approach to providing data subjects with access to their personal data and the ability to correct inaccurate data.*

**Statutory Requirement (s11):** A data subject shall be given access to his personal data held by a data user and be able to correct that personal data where the personal data is inaccurate, incomplete, misleading, or not up to date, except where compliance with a request to such access or correction is refused under this Act.

**Organisational Obligations:**

- The Organisation shall maintain a documented procedure for handling data subject access requests (DSARs) and correction requests (see Section 8 below).
- A data subject shall be provided access to their personal data within **21 days** of receipt of a valid written request, unless an extension or refusal is justified under the PDPA.
- Fees charged for processing access requests shall not exceed the limit prescribed by the Commissioner and shall be communicated to the data subject before processing the request.
- The Organisation shall correct inaccurate personal data within **21 days** of receipt of a valid correction request, or provide written reasons for any refusal to correct.
- Grounds for refusing access or correction shall be documented and communicated to the data subject in writing, together with information about the data subject's right to complain to the Commissioner.
- Records of all DSARs and correction requests, including the Organisation's response and the basis for any refusal, shall be maintained for a minimum of [X] years.

## 9. Data Breach Notification Procedures Overview

*This section provides an overview of the Organisation's obligations and procedures for managing and notifying personal data breaches. Full incident response procedures are contained in the Data Breach Response Plan referenced in Appendix G. Authors should ensure this section is consistent with the mandatory breach notification requirements introduced by Act A1727.*

### 9.1 Mandatory Breach Notification — Regulatory Background

The Personal Data Protection (Amendment) Act 2024 (Act A1727) introduced mandatory personal data breach notification obligations. The Organisation is required to notify the Commissioner and, in certain circumstances, affected data subjects where a personal data breach has occurred and meets the applicable notification threshold.

### 9.2 Definition of a Personal Data Breach

For the purposes of this Policy, a **personal data breach** is any security incident that results in:
- Unauthorised access to personal data.
- Unauthorised disclosure of personal data to a third party.
- Accidental or unlawful destruction, loss, alteration, or damage to personal data.
- Any other processing of personal data in a manner not authorised under this Policy or the PDPA.

### 9.3 Breach Notification Thresholds and Timelines

| Notification Obligation | Threshold | Timeline | Recipient |
|---|---|---|---|
| Notification to Commissioner | Breach that meets the threshold prescribed under Act A1727 (e.g., likely to result in significant harm to data subjects) | Within [72 hours / prescribed period] of becoming aware of the breach | Personal Data Protection Commissioner |
| Notification to affected data subjects | Breach that is likely to result in high risk to the rights and freedoms of data subjects | As soon as reasonably practicable after notification to Commissioner | Affected data subjects |

*Note: Authors must confirm the precise thresholds and timelines with reference to Act A1727 and any subsidiary regulations or guidance issued by the Commissioner, and update this table accordingly.*

### 9.4 Breach Response Procedure — Overview

**Phase 1 — Detection and Reporting:**
- All staff must report actual or suspected personal data breaches immediately to their line manager and to the Data Protection Officer.
- Reports may be made via [reporting channel — e.g., email to dpo@[organisation].com, helpdesk ticket, dedicated breach reporting hotline].
- Third-party processors must notify the Organisation of any personal data breach without undue delay in accordance with contractual obligations.

**Phase 2 — Containment and Assessment:**
- Upon receipt of a breach report, the DPO shall convene the **Data Breach Response Team** (comprising at minimum: DPO, CISO, Legal Counsel, and relevant Business Unit Head).
- The Response Team shall take immediate steps to contain the breach and prevent further loss or exposure.
- The DPO shall conduct an initial assessment within [X] hours to determine the scope, severity, and notification obligations arising from the breach.

**Phase 3 — Notification:**
- Where notification to the Commissioner is required, the DPO shall prepare and submit the breach notification within the prescribed timeline.
- Where notification to data subjects is required, the Organisation shall communicate with affected individuals in a clear, plain-language manner, including:
  - Description of the nature of the breach.
  - Categories and approximate number of personal data records and data subjects affected.
  - Contact details of the Data Protection Officer.
  - Likely consequences of the breach.
  - Measures taken or proposed to address the breach and mitigate its effects.
- Where direct notification to data subjects is not possible or would involve disproportionate effort, a public communication or equivalent measure may be used, as approved by the Commissioner.

**Phase 4 — Investigation and Remediation:**
- A full root cause analysis shall be conducted following containment of the breach.
- Remediation actions shall be documented and tracked to completion.
- The DPO shall submit a post-incident report to senior management and the Board within [X] days of the breach being resolved.

**Phase 5 — Documentation:**
- All personal data breaches, regardless of whether notification was required, shall be recorded in the **Data Breach Register** (Appendix H), including:
  - Date and time of detection.
  - Nature and description of the breach.
  - Categories and volume of personal data and data subjects affected.
  - Immediate actions taken.
  - Notification decisions and timelines.
  - Remediation actions and outcomes.

### 9.5 Breach Response Team
