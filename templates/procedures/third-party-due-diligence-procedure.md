## 1. Purpose and Scope

### 1.1 Purpose

This procedure establishes the mandatory requirements and standardized process by which [Organization Name] conducts privacy and data protection due diligence on all third parties prior to engagement, throughout the term of any engagement, and at offboarding. It ensures that [Organization Name] discharges its obligations as a **data controller** under the **Personal Data Protection Act 2010 (PDPA)**, specifically the requirements under **Section 9** (disclosure principle) and **Section 129A** (data processor obligations), by verifying that third parties handling personal data on behalf of [Organization Name] provide sufficient guarantees regarding technical and organizational security measures, and comply with applicable data protection obligations.

This procedure also supports [Organization Name]'s obligations under related regulatory frameworks, including Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM) guidelines on technology risk management and outsourcing, where applicable to financial institutions.

### 1.2 Scope

*This section defines the organizational and operational boundaries of this procedure. Confirm applicability with Legal and Compliance before finalizing.*

This procedure applies to:

- **All departments and business units** within [Organization Name] that engage, procure, or renew contracts with third parties who will access, process, store, transmit, or otherwise handle **personal data** as defined under the PDPA.
- **All third-party relationships**, including but not limited to:
  - Cloud service providers (SaaS, PaaS, IaaS)
  - IT and technology vendors and managed service providers
  - Payroll, HR, and benefits administrators
  - Marketing analytics and advertising partners
  - Legal, audit, and professional services firms
  - Courier, printing, and document management services
  - Data brokers and data analytics providers
  - Subcontractors and agents acting on behalf of [Organization Name]
- **All categories of personal data** processed by third parties, including but not limited to employee personal data, customer personal data, health data, and financial data.

### 1.3 Exclusions

*List any categories of third parties that are excluded from this procedure and the rationale, e.g., purely regulatory bodies, intra-group entities governed by a separate Group Data Transfer Agreement, etc.*

The following are **excluded** from the scope of this procedure:

| Excluded Category | Rationale | Alternative Control |
|---|---|---|
| [e.g., Intra-group entities covered by Group DPA] | [Governed by Group-level data transfer agreement] | [Group DPA, Group Privacy Policy] |
| [e.g., Purely regulatory and government bodies] | [Legal obligation; no commercial data processor relationship] | [Statutory disclosures log maintained by Legal] |
| [e.g., Third parties receiving anonymized/pseudonymized data] | [Personal data not involved post-anonymization] | [Anonymization standard verified by DPO] |

| Term | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Data Controller** | A person who either alone, jointly, or in common with other persons processes any personal data or has control over, or authorizes the processing of any personal data — in this procedure, [Organization Name]. |
| **Data Processor** | Any person who processes personal data solely on behalf of a data controller, and does not process personal data for any of their own purposes. (PDPA s.129A context) |

| **Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA)** | A structured assessment of the privacy risks associated with a specific processing activity or third-party engagement. |

## 4. Third-Party Classification

*This section establishes the classification framework for all third parties. Correct classification determines the applicable due diligence tier, contractual obligations, and ongoing oversight requirements. Classification is performed jointly by the DPO and the business unit initiating the engagement.*

### 4.1 Classification Categories

All third parties who will access or handle personal data on behalf of or in connection with [Organization Name] must be classified into one of the following categories before any engagement commences:

#### Category A — Data Processor

A Data Processor processes personal data **solely on behalf of [Organization Name]** and only upon [Organization Name]'s instructions. The Data Processor has no independent purpose or decision-making authority over the personal data.

**Examples:** Cloud infrastructure providers (IaaS/PaaS), payroll processing companies, data backup and archiving vendors, managed security service providers, call center operators.

**PDPA Obligation:** Section 129A applies in full. A Data Processing Agreement is mandatory. [Organization Name] remains the data controller and bears primary PDPA accountability.

#### Category B — Joint Data Controller

A Joint Data Controller jointly determines the **purposes and means of processing** personal data together with [Organization Name]. Both parties bear the obligations of a data controller under the PDPA.

**Examples:** Co-branded product partners, joint marketing campaign partners, consortium members sharing a common customer database, bancassurance partners.

**PDPA Obligation:** Both parties are data controllers. A Joint Controller/Data Controller Agreement defining each party's responsibilities is required. Each party must ensure independent PDPA compliance.

#### Category C — Independent Data Controller

An Independent Data Controller **receives personal data from [Organization Name]** and processes it entirely for their own purposes. Upon receipt, they become the data controller for that data. Disclosure to an Independent Data Controller must be lawful under Section 9 of the PDPA.

**Examples:** Credit reference agencies, regulatory and government bodies, law enforcement, insurers receiving claims data, lawyers acting for counterparties.

**PDPA Obligation:** Disclosure must be justified under PDPA s.9 (consent, legal obligation, or permitted exception). A Data Sharing Agreement or Disclosure Authorization is required. Ongoing oversight is limited but a record of disclosure must be maintained.

### 4.2 Classification Decision Matrix

*Use this matrix to guide classification. When in doubt, seek DPO guidance before proceeding.*

| Does the third party determine the purpose of processing? | Does the third party determine the means of processing? | Classification |
|---|---|---|
| No — [Organization Name] determines purpose | No — [Organization Name] determines means | **Category A: Data Processor** |
| Yes — jointly with [Organization Name] | Yes — jointly with [Organization Name] | **Category B: Joint Data Controller** |
| Yes — independently, for their own purposes | Yes — independently | **Category C: Independent Data Controller** |
| Uncertain | Uncertain | **Escalate to DPO for determination** |

## 5. Pre-Engagement Privacy Assessment

*This section describes the mandatory assessment process that must be completed before any contract is signed or access to personal data is granted. No third party classified under Categories A or B may be engaged without completing and documenting this assessment.*

### 5.1 Triggering the Assessment

The Pre-Engagement Privacy Assessment is triggered when:

- A business unit identifies a prospective vendor or partner who will access, process, store, or transmit personal data.
- A contract renewal or material amendment is proposed for an existing third party whose DPA or due diligence records have lapsed or are not current.
- A new service is added to an existing third-party relationship that introduces personal data processing not covered by the existing DPA.

**Trigger Notification:** The business unit **Relationship Owner** must submit a [Third-Party Engagement Intake Form — Appendix A] to the Procurement team and copy the DPO within **[10] business days** before the intended engagement commencement date.

### 5.2 Pre-Engagement Privacy Questionnaire (PEPQ)

*The PEPQ is the primary tool for gathering information about a prospective data processor's privacy and security posture. The full PEPQ template is provided in Appendix B. The following summarizes the mandatory sections.*

The DPO or a designated Privacy Analyst will issue the PEPQ to the prospective third party. The PEPQ must be completed and returned within **[15] business days** of issuance.

**PEPQ Mandatory Sections:**

| Section | Key Information Gathered |
|---|---|
| **Section 1 — Organization Profile** | Legal entity, jurisdiction of registration, group structure, key contact for data protection matters |
| **Section 2 — Data Protection Governance** | DPO appointment (if applicable), data protection policies, staff training program, privacy-by-design practices |
| **Section 3 — Data Inventory and Flows** | Categories of personal data to be processed, data flow mapping, countries of storage and processing, cross-border transfer mechanisms |
| **Section 4 — Security Controls** | Technical controls (encryption at rest and in transit, access control, MFA, logging), organizational controls (ISMS, security policies), physical security measures |
| **Section 5 — Incident Management** | Breach detection capabilities, incident response procedure, notification timelines, history of significant incidents in the past 3 years |
| **Section 6 — Subprocessors** | List of current subprocessors, nature of subprocessing, subprocessor security assessment process |
| **Section 7 — Audit and Compliance** | Current certifications (ISO 27001, SOC 2, etc.), last audit date and scope, willingness to submit to [Organization Name] audit rights |
| **Section 8 — Retention and Disposal** | Data retention policy, deletion/return process upon contract termination, certification of deletion capability |
| **Section 9 — Cross-Border Transfers** | Mechanisms used for cross-border personal data transfers, recipient countries, legal basis |

### 5.3 Privacy Risk Scoring

*Upon receipt of the completed PEPQ, the DPO conducts a risk assessment and assigns a risk tier. The scoring methodology and weightings should be defined and approved by the DPO and Risk Committee.*

Following receipt of the completed PEPQ, the DPO will conduct a structured Privacy Risk Assessment and assign a risk score and tier as follows:

| Risk Tier | Score Range | Description | Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Low Risk** | [0–30] | Minimal personal data, strong controls, no sensitive data | Standard DPA; annual review |
| **Tier 2 — Medium Risk** | [31–60] | Moderate personal data volume, adequate controls, limited sensitive data | Enhanced DPA; annual review with mid-year check |
| **Tier 3 — High Risk** | [61–80] | Significant personal data or sensitive data, controls need improvement | Enhanced DPA with specific remediations; semi-annual review; consider PIA |
| **Tier 4 — Critical Risk** | [81–100] | Large-scale sensitive personal data, inadequate controls, cross-border processing | DPO escalation required; Privacy Impact Assessment mandatory; engagement may be deferred or rejected |

**Assessment Criteria include:**

- Volume and sensitivity of personal data to be processed
- Jurisdictions involved (including cross-border data transfer risk)
- Nature of services (core processing vs. incidental access)
- Maturity of the vendor's data protection program
- History of incidents or regulatory actions
- Reliance on subprocessors
- Audit history and certification status

### 5.4 Privacy Impact Assessment (PIA) Threshold

A mandatory Privacy Impact Assessment must be conducted where:

- The prospective third party will process **sensitive personal data** on behalf of [Organization Name].
- The engagement involves **large-scale systematic processing** of personal data (threshold: [define threshold, e.g., >10,000 data subjects or >1,000 sensitive records]).
- The processing involves **cross-border transfer** of personal data to jurisdictions without adequate protection.
- The risk tier assessment results in **Tier 4 — Critical Risk**.
- The DPO determines a PIA is warranted based on the specific nature of the processing.

The PIA must be completed and approved by the DPO before the engagement proceeds. The PIA template and procedure are provided in [PIA Procedure — Document ID].

### 5.5 Assessment Outcomes

| Outcome | Conditions | Next Steps |
|---|---|---|
| **Approved — Proceed** | Tier 1 or Tier 2; PEPQ satisfactory | Proceed to DPA negotiation |
| **Approved with Conditions** | Tier 3; specific remediations required | Vendor must provide remediation plan; conditions incorporated into DPA; proceed to DPA negotiation |
| **Deferred — PIA Required** | Tier 4; PIA not yet completed | Complete PIA before proceeding |
| **Rejected** | Critical deficiencies; vendor unable or unwilling to remediate | Business unit notified; procurement halted; DPO to document rationale |

All assessment outcomes must be documented in the [Vendor Management System / SharePoint] and retained for a minimum of **[7] years** in accordance with the Records Management Policy.

## 6. Security and Compliance Certification Review

*This section governs the review of third-party security certifications and compliance attestations. Certifications provide independent evidence of a vendor's security posture and reduce the reliance on self-reported PEPQ responses. The DPO and Information Security team jointly conduct this review.*

### 6.1 Accepted Security Certifications

[Organization Name] recognizes the following certifications and attestations as evidence of compliance with information security standards applicable to personal data protection:

| Certification / Standard | Issuing Body | Relevance | Minimum Acceptable Scope |
|---|---|---|---|
| ISO/IEC 27001:2022 | ISO / Accredited CB | Information Security Management System | Must cover services provided to [Organization Name] |
| SOC 2 Type II | AICPA / Licensed CPA | Security, Availability, Confidentiality, Processing Integrity | Type II preferred over Type I; report must be < 12 months old |
| CSA STAR Level 2 | Cloud Security Alliance | Cloud-specific security controls | Applicable to cloud service providers |
| PCI DSS (if applicable) | PCI SSC | Payment card data security | Required where cardholder data is in scope |
| MyDIGITAL / NACSA Certification | NACSA | Malaysian government cloud security | Applicable to government-sector cloud providers |
| ISO/IEC 27701:2019 | ISO / Accredited CB | Privacy Information Management (extension to ISO 27001) | Strongly preferred for data processors handling large volumes of personal data |
| [Other — specify] | [Issuing Body] | [Relevance] | [Scope requirement] |

### 6.2 Certification Review Process

The Information Security team and DPO will:

1. **Request** current certificates, audit reports, and attestation letters directly from the vendor.
2. **Verify authenticity** of certificates by cross-referencing with the issuing certification body's public register where available (e.g., ISO CB directory, AICPA SOC report registry).
3. **Review scope** to confirm the certification scope covers the systems and services that will process [Organization Name]'s personal data.
4. **Assess findings** — for SOC 2 and similar reports, review exceptions and qualified opinions; material exceptions must be escalated to the DPO.
5. **Check currency** — certifications must not be expired; certificates older than **12 months** are not accepted unless the vendor provides evidence that a surveillance or recertification audit is in progress.
6. **Document** the outcome in the Third-Party Certification Register (Appendix C).

### 6.3 Certification Gaps and Compensating Controls

*Where a prospective vendor does not hold a recognized certification, compensating controls must be documented and approved by the DPO and CISO.*

Where a vendor does not hold an accepted certification, [Organization Name] may accept **compensating controls** at the DPO's and CISO's discretion, subject to:

| Compensating Control | Acceptability Criteria |
|---|---|
| Penetration testing report (< 12 months) | Must be conducted by an independent, qualified party; critical findings must be remediated |
| Internal audit report on information security | Must be conducted by qualified internal auditors; scope must be adequate; material findings remediated |
| Self-assessment against ISO 27001 or NIST CSF | Accepted only for Tier 1 Low Risk vendors; DPO must review and accept self-assessment |
| Vendor-specific security questionnaire responses | Cross-referenced against PEPQ; subject to spot-check or onsite review |

Compensating controls must be documented in the vendor's due diligence file and reviewed at each annual reassessment.

## 7. Data Processing Agreement Requirements and Negotiation

*This section defines the mandatory contractual requirements for Data Processing Agreements and the process for negotiating, executing, and maintaining DPAs with data processors (Category A) and Joint Data Controller Agreements with Category B third parties.*

### 7.1 Mandatory DPA Requirement

Pursuant to **PDPA Section 129A**, **no personal data may be transferred to or processed by a data processor** before a binding Data Processing Agreement is executed between [Organization Name] and the data processor. This is a non-negotiable requirement.

**No exceptions are permitted without the written approval of the DPO and Group Legal.**

### 7.2 Mandatory DPA Clauses

All DPAs executed by [Organization Name] must include, at minimum, the following clauses. The DPO and Legal are jointly responsible for maintaining a standard DPA template (Appendix D) incorporating these requirements.

| Clause | Mandatory Content |
|---|---|
| **Subject Matter and Duration** | Precise description of the processing activities; duration aligned with the main services agreement |
| **Nature and Purpose of Processing** | Explicit statement of the purposes for which personal data is processed; instruction-only processing requirement |
| **Type of Personal Data and Data Subjects** | Categories of personal data; categories of data subjects affected |
| **Data Controller Obligations and Rights** | [Organization Name]'s rights to issue instructions, conduct audits, approve subprocessors, and terminate processing |
| **Processor Obligations** | Process only on documented instructions; confidentiality obligation on authorized personnel; security measures (referencing s.10 PDPA); subprocessor restrictions; assistance with data subject rights; assistance with breach notification; deletion/return on termination |
| **Security Measures** | Minimum technical and organizational measures, aligned with ISO 27001 or equivalent; encryption standards; access control requirements |
| **Subprocessor Restrictions** | Prior written consent of [Organization Name] required before engaging subprocessors; flow-down of DPA obligations |
| **Data Subject Rights Assistance** | Processor to assist [Organization Name] in responding to data subject access, correction, and objection requests within prescribed timescales |
| **Breach Notification** | Notification to [Organization Name] within **[24/48/72] hours** of becoming aware of a personal data breach; content requirements for notification |
| **Audit Rights** | [Organization Name]'s right to conduct audits and inspections; cooperation requirements |
| **International Transfers** | Restriction on cross-border transfer of personal data without prior written consent; approved transfer mechanisms |
| **Retention and Deletion** | Data retention schedule; deletion or return of all personal data at contract termination; written certification |
| **Governing Law** | Laws of Malaysia; submission to Malaysian jurisdiction |
| **Liability and Indemnity** | Processor liability for breaches of DPA; indemnity provisions |

### 7.3 DPA Negotiation Process

*This process governs how DPAs are negotiated with third parties who propose their own template DPAs or who push back on [Organization Name]'s standard terms.*

**Step 1 — Issue Standard DPA Template**
Legal issues [Organization Name]'s standard DPA template to the prospective data processor at the outset of contract negotiations.

**Step 2 — Review Third-Party Counter-Proposal**
If the vendor proposes amendments or a counter-template, Legal and the DPO review the proposed changes against the mandatory clause requirements in Section 7.2.

**Step 3 — Assess Acceptability of Amendments**

| Vendor Amendment Category | Action |
|---|---|
| Administrative / formatting changes | Legal may accept at discretion |
| Changes to security standard references (e.g., alternative ISO standard) | DPO and CISO to review and approve |
| Changes to notification timelines (breach notification) | DPO must approve; 72 hours is the maximum acceptable timeframe |
| Removal or dilution of audit rights | Escalate to DPO; not acceptable without compensating mechanism |
| Removal of subprocessor restrictions | Not acceptable; reject |
| Governing law changes (non-Malaysian) | Escalate to Group Legal; DPO to assess regulatory impact |
| Limitation of liability below [specified threshold] | Escalate to Group Legal and Risk Committee |

**Step 4 — Escalation for Unresolved Issues**
Unresolved material issues must be escalated to the DPO and Group Legal within **[5] business days**. If the vendor is unwilling to accept mandatory terms, the DPO may recommend that the engagement be rejected.

**Step 5 — DPA Execution**
Executed DPAs must be:
- Signed by an authorized signatory of both parties.
- Stored in [Contract Management System / Location] with a copy in the vendor's due diligence file.
- Registered in the Third-Party DPA Register (maintained by Legal / DPO).

### 7.5 Joint Data Controller Agreements (Category B)

For **Category B — Joint Data Controllers**, a Joint Data Controller Agreement (JDUA) must be executed in lieu of a DPA. The JDUA must:

- Identify which party acts as the primary point of contact for data subjects.

## 8. Subprocessor Disclosure and Approval Process

*This section governs [Organization Name]'s rights and process for reviewing, approving, and monitoring the use of subprocessors by data processors engaged under a DPA. Subprocessor management is critical to maintaining the chain of accountability for personal data under PDPA s.129A.*

### 8.1 Subprocessor Consent Requirement

**No data processor may engage a subprocessor to process [Organization Name]'s personal data without [Organization Name]'s prior written consent.** This requirement must be explicitly stated in all DPAs.

[Organization Name] may grant consent either:

- **Specifically** — by approving a named subprocessor for a defined scope of processing; or
- **Generally** — by approving a class of subprocessors subject to notification and objection rights (permitted only where the DPO determines this is appropriate based on risk).

### 8.2 Subprocessor Disclosure at Onboarding

At the time of DPA execution, the data processor must provide a complete and current **Subprocessor Disclosure List** (see template at Appendix E) containing:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Subprocessor Name | Legal entity name of the subprocessor |
| Jurisdiction | Country of incorporation and country where processing occurs |
| Processing Activities | Nature of personal data processed and purposes |
| Personal Data Categories | Types of personal data accessed or processed |
| Security Certifications | Relevant certifications held by the subprocessor |
| Transfer Mechanism | Cross-border transfer mechanism if data leaves Malaysia |

### 8.3 Subprocessor Change Notification and Approval

Where a data processor intends to:

- **Engage a new subprocessor**, or
- **Replace an existing approved subprocessor**, or
- **Materially change the scope of an existing subprocessor's processing activities**

the data processor must:

1. Provide [Organization Name] with **written notice** at least **[30] calendar days** before the intended change.
2. Include sufficient information for [Organization Name] to conduct a risk assessment of the new or changed subprocessing arrangement.
3. Allow [Organization Name] the right to **object** to the change within **[30] calendar days** of receipt of notice.

**Objection Procedure:**
- Objections must be submitted in writing to the data processor and copied to the DPO.
- Where [Organization Name] objects, the data processor must not proceed with the change until the objection is resolved.
- If the parties cannot resolve the objection, [Organization Name] reserves the right to terminate the DPA without penalty.

### 8.4 Subprocessor Risk Assessment

Upon receiving notice of a new or changed subprocessor, the DPO will conduct a risk assessment within **[15] business days**, assessing:

- **Jurisdictional risk** — is the subprocessor based in or processing data in a country with adequate data protection?
- **Security posture** — does the subprocessor hold equivalent certifications to those required of the primary data processor?
- **DPA flow-down** — has the data processor confirmed that DPA obligations are flowed down to the subprocessor by contract?
- **Concentration risk** — does the subprocessor relationship create an unacceptable concentration of processing risk (e.g., a single subprocessor processing data for multiple of [Organization Name]'s primary processors)?

### 8.5 Subprocessor Register

## 9. Annual Reassessment Triggers and Methodology

*This section defines the ongoing oversight obligations for active third-party relationships. Reassessment ensures that [Organization Name]'s data protection risk posture remains current and that material changes in the vendor's environment are identified promptly.*

### 9.1 Scheduled Annual Reassessment

All active data processors (Category A) and joint data controllers (Category B) with access to personal data must undergo a **formal reassessment at least annually**. The reassessment cycle is managed by the DPO in coordination with the Relationship Owner.

**Annual Reassessment Schedule:**
- The DPO publishes an Annual Reassessment Calendar by **[1 February]** of each calendar year.
- Relationship Owners are notified **[60] calendar days** before the reassessment due date.
- Vendors are issued the Reassessment Questionnaire **[45] calendar days** before the due date.
- Completed reassessments must be documented and signed off by the DPO by the due date.

### 9.2 Event-Driven Reassessment Triggers

In addition to the scheduled annual reassessment, an **immediate unscheduled reassessment** must be initiated when any of the following trigger events occur:

| Trigger Event | Responsible Party for Notification | Target Completion |
|---|---|---|
| Third-party personal data breach (actual or suspected) | Data Processor (per DPA breach notification clause) | Within **[5] business days** of breach notification |
| Significant change in third-party ownership, merger, or acquisition | Relationship Owner / Legal | Within **[20] business days** of confirmed change |
| Regulatory action or enforcement notice against the third party | Relationship Owner / Legal | Within **[10] business days** of becoming aware |
| Loss or suspension of a key security certification | Data Processor (per DPA) | Within **[10] business days** of becoming aware |
| Material change in scope of services or personal data processed | Relationship Owner | Prior to change taking effect |
| Adverse media, reputational event, or material complaint concerning the third party | Relationship Owner / Procurement | Within **[10] business days** of becoming aware |
| Change in applicable law affecting the third party's processing | DPO / Legal | Within **[20] business days** of law taking effect |
| Expiry of DPA without renewal | DPO / Legal | Immediately upon expiry — processing must cease until renewed |

### 9.3 Reassessment Methodology

The annual and event-driven reassessment process follows these steps:

**Step 1 — Pre-Assessment Preparation**
The DPO or Privacy Analyst reviews the vendor's current due diligence file, the previous assessment report, and any events or changes noted since the last assessment.

**Step 2 — Reassessment Questionnaire**
A targeted Reassessment Questionnaire (abbreviated version of the PEPQ, focused on changes since the last assessment) is issued to the vendor. The questionnaire must address:
- Changes to data protection governance (DPO, policies, training)
- Changes to systems, infrastructure, or subprocessors used to process [Organization Name]'s data
- Security incidents or near-misses since last assessment
- Changes to certifications
- Regulatory interactions or enforcement actions
- Changes to cross-border processing arrangements

**Step 3 — Documentation Review**
Updated certifications, audit reports, and any relevant supporting documentation are reviewed.

**Step 4 — Risk Tier Review**
The DPO reviews the vendor's risk tier assignment and updates it if warranted. A change in risk tier may trigger enhanced due diligence or an onsite audit.

**Step 5 — Remediation Tracking**
Outstanding remediations from prior assessments are tracked. Non-completion of agreed remediations within the agreed timeframe is escalated to Procurement and the Risk Committee.

**Step 6 — Assessment Sign-Off**
The DPO documents the assessment outcome, updates the Third-Party Register, and issues a formal assessment completion notice to the Relationship Owner.

### 9.4 Reassessment Outcomes

| Outcome | Description | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Continued — No Issues** | Assessment satisfactory; no material changes | Update register; schedule next annual assessment |
| **Continued — Minor Issues** | Minor gaps identified; vendor to provide remediation plan | Remediation plan required within **[30] days**; tracked by DPO |
| **Continued — Conditions** | Material issues identified; engagement continues subject to conditions | Conditions incorporated as DPA amendment; monitoring enhanced |
| **Escalated — Urgent Remediation** | Significant risk identified; immediate action required | DPO and Risk Committee notified; vendor given **[15] business days** to remediate or face suspension |
| **Suspended** | Processing suspended pending remediation or investigation | DPO issues suspension notice; data access revoked; Relationship Owner notified |
| **Terminated** | Vendor unable or unwilling to remediate; relationship ended | Offboarding procedure initiated (Section 11) |

## 10. Onsite Audit Rights Exercise Procedure

*This section defines the process by which [Organization Name] exercises its audit rights over data processors pursuant to the DPA. Onsite audits provide the highest level of assurance over vendor compliance and are typically reserved for high-risk or critical-risk vendors or where specific concerns exist. All DPAs must include audit rights as a mandatory clause.*

### 10.1 Basis for Onsite Audit Rights

[Organization Name]'s right to conduct onsite audits of data processors must be explicitly stated in the DPA. The DPA should provide that [Organization Name] may:

- Conduct audits **at its own expense** with reasonable advance notice (typically **[30] calendar days**).
- Commission a qualified **independent third party** to conduct audits on its behalf.
- Receive and review audit reports, including penetration test results, SOC 2 reports, and internal audit findings, in lieu of direct onsite audit (where mutually agreed and the DPO accepts this as adequate).

### 10.2 Trigger Criteria for Onsite Audit

An onsite audit may be initiated under the following circumstances:

| Trigger | Priority |
|---|---|
| Vendor risk tier is **Tier 3 (High Risk) or Tier 4 (Critical Risk)** and no third-party audit report is available | High |
| A personal data breach has occurred at the vendor's premises or systems | Urgent |
| Annual reassessment reveals significant unresolved gaps | High |
| Regulatory inquiry or enforcement action involving the vendor | High |
| Vendor has failed to provide updated certifications within [30] days of expiry | Medium |
| DPO determines an audit is warranted based on specific intelligence or concerns | As determined |
| Routine assurance audit per the annual audit plan | Low (planned) |

### 10.3 Onsite Audit Planning

**Step 1 — Audit Authorization**
The DPO prepares an Audit Authorization Memo, approved by the [CISO / CRO / relevant executive], specifying:
- Audit objectives and scope
- Audit team composition (internal and/or external auditors)
- Proposed audit dates
- Data categories and systems to be reviewed

**Step 2 — Notice to Vendor**
Written audit notice is issued to the vendor at least **[30] calendar days** in advance (unless the audit is initiated in response to a breach or urgent event, in which case shorter notice may be given, subject to DPA terms). The notice must specify:
- Proposed dates and duration
- Scope of audit
- Logistics and access requirements
- Information and documentation to be prepared by the vendor

**Step 3 — Audit Preparation**
The audit team reviews:
- Prior assessment reports and due diligence file
- DPA and any amendments
- Vendor's responses to the most recent PEPQ or Reassessment Questionnaire
- Any known incidents or concerns

**Step 4 — Onsite Audit Execution**
The audit typically covers the following areas:

| Audit Domain | Illustrative Checks |
|---|---|
| Data Protection Governance | DPO appointment, policy framework, staff awareness training completion rates |
| Access Control | Principle of least privilege; MFA enforcement; access review logs; termination procedures |
| Encryption | Encryption at rest and in transit; key management procedures |
| Incident Response | Breach response plan; drill records; notification procedures |
| Subprocessor Management | Contracts with subprocessors; security assessments of subprocessors |
| Data Inventory | Accuracy of data inventory; data minimization practices |
| Retention and Disposal | Retention schedules; deletion procedures; certificates of destruction |
| Physical Security | Server room access controls; visitor logs; clean desk compliance |
| Audit Logs | Log completeness; integrity; retention period |
| DPA Compliance | Evidence of processing only on instruction; training of processor personnel on DPA obligations |

**Step 5 — Audit Report**
The audit team prepares a formal Audit Report within **[15] business days** of completion, containing:
- Executive summary
- Scope and methodology
- Findings and risk ratings (Critical / High / Medium / Low / Informational)
- Agreed remediation actions and target completion dates
- Overall assessment opinion

**Step 6 — Remediation Follow-Up**
The DPO tracks all remediation actions agreed in the Audit Report. Vendors must provide evidence of remediation by the agreed target dates. Critical and High findings not remediated within **[30] business days** are escalated to the Risk Committee.

### 10.4 Audit Documentation and Retention

All audit documentation, including the Audit Authorization Memo, audit plan, working papers, and Audit Report, must be retained for a minimum of **[7] years** in accordance with the Records Management Policy and stored in [Audit Management System / Location].

## 11. Offboarding: Data Return, Deletion, and Certification

*This section governs the structured offboarding of data processors at the end of their engagement, ensuring that all personal data held by the data processor is returned to [Organization Name] or securely destroyed, and that [Organization Name] obtains written certification of such return or destruction. This is a mandatory obligation under the PDPA retention principle (s.11) and the DPA.*

### 11.1 Offboarding Triggers

Offboarding of a data processor is triggered by:

- Expiry or termination of the services agreement or DPA
- Termination for cause (e.g., material breach, non-remediated audit findings)
- [Organization Name]'s decision to change vendors or bring the service in-house
- Vendor insolvency, winding up, or cessation of business
- Regulatory direction requiring cessation of the relationship

### 11.2 Offboarding Initiation

Upon triggering an offboarding, the Relationship Owner must:

1. Notify the DPO and Procurement **immediately** (and no later than **[5] business days** before intended termination, unless termination is immediate).
2. Issue a formal **Offboarding Initiation Notice** to the vendor, specifying:
   - Effective date of termination
   - Inventory of personal data categories held by the vendor
   - Instructions for data return or deletion (as determined by the DPO)
   - Required timeline for completion
   - Certification requirements

### 11.3 Data Inventory and Return or Deletion Decision

**Step 1 — Data Inventory Confirmation**
The vendor must provide a complete **Data Inventory Declaration** (Appendix F) within **[10] business days** of receiving the Offboarding Initiation Notice, confirming:
- All personal data held (by category and approximate volume)
- All locations (primary, backup, disaster recovery, subprocessors)
- All formats (electronic, paper, other physical media)

**Step 2 — DPO Decision: Return or Deletion**

| Option | Conditions | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Return of Data** | [Organization Name] requires the data for ongoing operations or regulatory purposes | Vendor exports data in an agreed format (e.g., structured CSV, encrypted archive) and transfers it to [Organization Name] via secure, encrypted channel within **[20] business days** |
| **Secure Deletion** | Data is no longer required by [Organization Name]; retention period has expired or will expire | Vendor securely deletes all copies (including backups and subprocessor copies) using [approved deletion standard, e.g., NIST 800-88 / DoD 5220.22-M] within **[30] business days** |
| **Combination** | Some data returned; some deleted | DPO specifies which data is to be returned and which deleted |

**Step 3 — Subprocessor Data**
The vendor must confirm that all subprocessors holding [Organization Name]'s personal data have also completed the return or deletion, and must include subprocessor compliance in the Certificate of Data Return / Deletion.

### 11.4 Offboarding Checklist

*The Relationship Owner and DPO must jointly complete the Offboarding Checklist (Appendix G) to confirm all offboarding steps have been completed.*

| # | Offboarding Action | Responsible | Target Date | Completion Date | Confirmed By |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Offboarding Initiation Notice issued to vendor | Relationship Owner | [Date] | [Date] | [Name] |
| 2 | Data Inventory Declaration received from vendor | DPO | [Date + 10 days] | [Date] | [Name] |
| 3 | DPO decision: return or deletion confirmed in writing | DPO | [Date] | [Date] | [Name] |
| 4 | System / API access revoked | IT Security | [Effective Date] | [Date] | [Name] |
| 5 | Physical access (if any) revoked and credentials returned | Facilities / IT Security | [Effective Date] | [Date] | [Name] |
| 6 | Data returned (if applicable) and integrity verified | DPO / IT | [Date + 20 days] | [Date] | [Name] |
| 7 | Data deletion completed (primary systems) | Vendor | [Date + 30 days] | [Date] | [Name] |

### 11.5 Certificate of Data Return / Deletion

*The vendor must provide a formal Certificate of Data Return / Deletion (Appendix H) signed by an authorized representative of the vendor. This certificate is a mandatory document and must be retained for [7] years.*

The Certificate must confirm:

- All personal data held by the vendor (including backups, DR copies, and archival copies) has been returned or securely deleted.
- All subprocessors have similarly returned or deleted all personal data.
- The deletion method used meets the standard specified by [Organization Name].
- No copies of [Organization Name]'s personal data are retained by the vendor or any subprocessor.

If the vendor is unable to certify complete deletion (e.g., due to backup rotation cycles not yet completing), the vendor must specify the timeline by which deletion will be complete and provide a supplementary certificate upon completion.

### 11.6 Offboarding Records Retention

All offboarding documentation — including the Offboarding Initiation Notice, Data Inventory Declaration, return or deletion instructions, Certificate of Data Return / Deletion, and the completed Offboarding Checklist — must be retained for a minimum of **[7] years** in accordance with the Records Management Policy and stored in [Document Management System / Location].

### 12.2 Key Roles — Detailed Responsibilities

**Data Protection Officer (DPO)**
- Owns the Third-Party Due Diligence Procedure and all associated registers.
- Provides binding privacy risk assessments and classification decisions.
- Approves or rejects prospective engagements based on TPDD outcomes.
- Oversees DPA negotiation from a privacy compliance perspective.
- Approves subprocessor engagements.
- Manages the annual reassessment calendar.
- Signs off all assessment and audit reports.

**Information Security (CISO / Information Security Team)**
- Reviews vendor security certifications and audit reports.
- Participates in onsite audit execution for technical security domains.
- Advises on minimum security control requirements in DPAs.
- Reviews and approves compensating controls where certifications are absent.

**Legal**
- Drafts, reviews, and negotiates DPAs and Joint Data Controller Agreements.
- Advises on cross-border data transfer mechanisms and legality.
- Maintains the DPA register.
- Provides legal advice on offboarding disputes and data deletion obligations.

**Procurement**
- Ensures TPDD requirements are integrated into the procurement process and vendor selection criteria.
- Confirms DPA execution before contract award.
- Coordinates with Relationship Owners on vendor onboarding timelines.

**Relationship Owner (Business Unit)**
- Initiates the TPDD process by submitting the Intake Form.
- Acts as primary liaison with the vendor during the assessment and offboarding process.
- Monitors ongoing vendor performance and reports trigger events to the DPO.

1. Relationship Owner submits a written **Exception Request** to the DPO, including justification and proposed compensating controls.
2. DPO assesses the risk and, where the exception involves a statutory requirement or high-risk matter, consults Legal and/or the Risk Committee.
3. Approved exceptions are documented in the Exception Register (maintained by DPO) with an expiry date and review date.
